COMPUTATIONAL Trial

A primary care-based cluster randomised trial of personalised renal function monitoring and interventions in people living with heart failure

About the Trial

Almost one million people in the UK live with heart failure, with almost 50% also experiencing kidney (renal) problems. The heart and kidneys are closely interconnected: if the heart suddenly becomes less efficient, renal function can worsen. Modern treatments enable people with heart failure to live longer, healthier lives, but their medications must be carefully monitored. Too low a dose of heart medicine can worsen heart failure symptoms, while higher doses may impair renal function. Both situations can increase the risk of hospital admission.

The COMPUTATIONAL trial will explore the clinical and cost-effectiveness of a personalised renal function monitoring algorithm, designed to prevent hospital admissions due to renal impairment without worsening heart failure outcomes.

  • The algorithm, classed as a medical device, consists of a prediction model designed to tailor monitoring schedules, together with a decision tool that provides guidance on immediate interventions and recommendations for the primary care team.
  • We will recruit 200 GP practices across England that use the OPTUM (EMIS) system, evaluating around 20,000 patients. 100 centres will be randomised to use the prediction model, while the other 100 will be randomised to continue with standard care.
  • Patient Reported Outcome Measures (PROMs) will also be collected from 130 practices (65 in each group) at baseline, 6- and 18-months.
  • The trial will run for 18 months, in line with National Institute for Health and Care Excellence (NICE) guidelines and current standards of care, after which all participants will return to usual care for patients with heart failure.

Trial Objectives

Primary objectives:

  1. To compare the clinical effectiveness of personalised renal function monitoring with standard care in patients living with heart failure.

Secondary objectives:

  1. To determine if implementation of personalised renal function monitoring in patients living with heart failure improves a variety of clinical outcomes, when compared with standard care.
  2. To identify barriers in the optimal use of personalised renal function monitoring for both the primary care team and patients.

Economic objective:

  1. To estimate the cost-effectiveness of personalised renal function monitoring in comparison to standard care.
