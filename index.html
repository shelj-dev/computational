Background â€“ Heart Failure & Kidney Connection

Almost one million people in the UK live with heart failure, with almost 50% also experiencing kidney (renal) problems. The heart and kidneys are closely interconnected: if the heart suddenly becomes less efficient, renal function can worsen. Modern treatments enable people with heart failure to live longer, healthier lives, but their medications must be carefully monitored. Too low a dose of heart medicine can worsen heart failure symptoms, while higher doses may impair renal function. Both situations can increase the risk of hospital admission.

Clinical Trial Objectives & Key Outcomes

Primary Objective

To compare the clinical effectiveness of personalised renal function monitoring with standard care in patients living with heart failure.

Secondary Objectives

To determine if personalised monitoring improves outcomes in heart failure patients compared with standard care.

Barriers to Use

To identify barriers in the optimal use of personalised renal function monitoring for both primary care teams and patients.

Economic Objective

To estimate the cost-effectiveness of personalised renal function monitoring compared to standard care.

Trial Summary Overview

The COMPUTATIONAL trial explores the clinical and cost-effectiveness of a personalised renal function monitoring algorithm.

Main Trial Focus

The algorithm, classed as a medical device, consists of a prediction model designed to tailor monitoring schedules, together with a decision tool that provides guidance on immediate interventions and recommendations for the primary care team.

We will recruit 200 GP practices across England that use the OPTUM (EMIS) system, evaluating around 20,000 patients. 100 centres will be randomised to use the prediction model, while the other 100 will be randomised to continue with standard care.

  • Patient Reported Outcomes

    PROMs will be collected from 130 practices (65 in each group) at baseline, 6- and 18-months.

  • Trial Duration

    The trial will run for 18 months, in line with NICE guidelines and current standards of care, after which all participants will return to usual care for patients with heart failure.
